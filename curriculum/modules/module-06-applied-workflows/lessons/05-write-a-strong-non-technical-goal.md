# Lesson 6.05: Write A Strong Non-Technical Goal

## Learn

Good Goals are specified before Codex starts working. That does not mean you need perfect wording right away. It means you should ask Codex to help shape the Goal before you activate it.

A strong non-technical Goal gives Codex enough structure to keep moving without wandering. It should say what the finished artifact should look like, what evidence Codex should use, what sources or files are allowed, what must be preserved, how uncertainty should be labeled, and when Codex should stop to ask for help.

Good Goal candidates often involve careful investigation or an audit trail, such as:

- audit workflow with an audit trail
- vendor evaluation
- market landscape research
- candidate sourcing and analysis
- due diligence review
- literature review
- policy or process comparison
- large document review with evidence tagging

Weak Goal:

```text
/goal Research vendors for us.
```

Stronger Goal:

```text
/goal Create an evidence-backed vendor evaluation for the three tools named in this folder. Use only the source notes, vendor websites, and approved comparison criteria. End with a table of findings, source links, confidence levels, risks, and open questions. If a required source is missing or a claim cannot be verified, label it as blocked or uncertain instead of filling the gap.
```

Notice the difference. The stronger Goal tells Codex what to produce, where evidence can come from, what not to invent, and how to handle missing information.

Before activating a Goal, it is smart to have Codex interview you. The interview should answer:

- What outcome do you want?
- What evidence would prove it?
- What files, folders, or sources can Codex use?
- What should Codex avoid?
- What should count as done?
- What should count as blocked?
- How should Codex label uncertainty?

That interview step is the learner's steering wheel. It keeps Goal mode useful without turning it into vague momentum.

## Practice

Try this in Codex:

```text
Help me turn this workflow into a strong Codex Goal. Interview me first. Then draft a Goal that includes the desired outcome, evidence source, constraints, allowed inputs, iteration approach, and blocked stop condition. Focus on a non-technical work use case, not coding.
```

**Success criteria:** The learner ends with a Goal that is specific enough to verify, names what "done," "blocked," and "uncertain" mean, and includes boundaries so Codex does not wander into unsupported work.
